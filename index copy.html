Multisensory Continual Learning: Adapting Pretrained Visuomotor Policies to Force

Anonymous Authors

Adding new sensor modalities is key for contact-rich tasks, but we lack the data to scale multisensory foundation models. What if all our data doesn't need to contain multisensory observations? With MuSe, multitask visuomotor policies learn new modalities during finetuning with only a fraction of the pretraining data.

  • MuSe leverages multisensory inputs for contact-rich interaction and improves visual generalization from pretraining, indicating positive forward transfer.
  • MuSe demonstrates backward transfer, leveraging the new modality to improve on pretraining tasks that do not exist in the minimal finetuning stage.
  • MuSe provides a pathway to training large-scale multisensory robot policies and world models with practical data composition: large amounts of visual pretraining data, robot vision-action data, and a small amount of diverse multisensory data such as force, tactile, or audio.

Method

Incorporating new modalities requires the model to reason over new input dimensions, without either over-relying on or ignoring them. This requires an algorithm that avoids catastrophic forgetting of knowledge acquired during pretraining while properly incorporating new input modalities through an effective cross-modal representation. We design MuSe to achieve this using three key mechanisms:

  • Multi-stage fusion: Force histories are encoded into the same token space as images, proprioception, language, and actions for early transformer-level interaction, then injected again through late cross-attention adapters.
  • Multisensory Prediction Targets: The model predicts future actions, future visual observations, and future F/T signals, encouraging a shared representation grounded in both visual dynamics and contact.
  • Episodic recall: Finetuning mixes new multisensory data with the original pretraining data. Missing force inputs are masked, preserving old skills while adding the new modality.

Dataset and Key Results

+35% forward contact-rich transfer

On contact-rich finetuned tasks, MuSe improves from 46.7% to 81.7% success over the No F/T input baseline.

+20% improved backward transfer

On contact-rich tasks from pretraining, MuSe improves from 55.0% to 75.0% success over a pretrained vision-only policy.

MuSe predicts force where force was never supervised.

After learning from limited multisensory finetuning data, MuSe can predict F/T traces on pretraining tasks that only used vision-action supervision.

  • Shared multisensory representation: F/T prediction on unlabeled pretraining tasks suggests the model learns a shared multisensory representation that transfers beyond the finetuning distribution.
  • Adaptive compliance at runtime: predicted future F/T provides a contact profile that can set compliance targets during deployment.

Why F/T?

Force-torque sensing exposes physical interactions that images cannot capture

MuSe uses F/T in two complementary ways. As an input, force histories tell the policy when contact has started, whether the robot is pushing too hard, and when a peg has jammed. As an output, predicted future F/T gives the controller an expected contact profile that can be converted into adaptive compliance targets, so the robot can stay stiff in free space and become compliant when contact is anticipated.

We find that both F/T input and output is essential for contact-rich tasks. Wiping needs sufficient normal force to erase marks without hitting safety limits, while peg insertion needs to leverage force for recovery from friction and jams rather than blindly pushing.
